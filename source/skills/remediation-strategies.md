# Dyslexia Remediation Strategies Skill

## Purpose
Provide evidence-based remediation strategies, intervention plans, and instructional guidance for dyslexia support — grounded in structured literacy principles, Orton-Gillingham methodology, and the IDA Knowledge and Practice Standards.

> **Cognitive Disorder Framing**: This skill treats dyslexia as a **neurodevelopmental cognitive disorder** — a persistent, brain-based difference in phonological and orthographic processing architecture centered on the left temporoparietal cortex, visual word form area (VWFA), and inferior frontal gyrus (Shaywitz, Dehaene, Gabrieli). This distinction matters: accommodations should compensate for a persistent cognitive processing difference, not merely scaffold a temporary learning gap that resolves with sufficient instruction.

## Core Knowledge Base

### Structured Literacy: The Six Elements
All remediation must address one or more of these interconnected elements:

1. **Phonology** — Sound structure of spoken words. Activities: rhyming, syllable counting, phoneme segmentation/blending/manipulation.
2. **Sound-Symbol Association (Phonics)** — Bidirectional mapping of phonemes to graphemes. Must be taught both for reading (visual → auditory) and spelling (auditory → visual).
3. **Syllable Instruction** — Systematic teaching of syllable types (closed, open, VCe, vowel team, r-controlled, consonant-le) for decoding multisyllabic words.
4. **Morphology** — Study of morphemes (prefixes, suffixes, roots, base words). Essential for vocabulary expansion and spelling accuracy.
5. **Syntax** — Sentence-level grammar and mechanics. Supports reading comprehension and written expression.
6. **Semantics** — Meaning-making through vocabulary development and comprehension strategies.

### Orton-Gillingham Principles
All remediation strategies should follow these principles:

- **Multisensory**: Engage sight, hearing, touch, and movement simultaneously (e.g., tracing letters while saying sounds, tapping phonemes, using letter tiles).
- **Direct and Explicit**: Teach every skill explicitly. Never assume students will infer patterns.
- **Diagnostic and Prescriptive**: Continuously assess understanding. Adapt instruction based on what the student demonstrates.
- **Sequential and Cumulative**: Build on previously mastered skills. Every lesson reviews prior content before introducing new material.
- **Individualized**: Tailor pacing and content to the student's specific profile and progress.

### Evidence-Based Programs Reference

| Program | Foundation | Best For | Intensity |
|---------|-----------|----------|-----------|
| **Wilson Reading System** | OG-based, 12-step systematic phonics | Grades 2-12, non-responders with word-level deficits | 3-5x/week, 30-50 min |
| **Barton Reading & Spelling** | OG-based, 10 levels | Homeschool, small group, paraprofessional delivery | Flexible scheduling |
| **Lindamood-Bell (LiPS)** | Sensory-cognitive, symbol/concept imagery | Phonological processing weaknesses | 1-2 hrs daily, 4-6 weeks intensive |
| **Sonday System** | OG multisensory | Digital or traditional delivery | Flexible |
| **RAVE-O** | Fluency-focused supplement | Accurate but slow decoders | Used alongside decoding programs |
| **Alphabetic Phonics** | OG-Childs extension | Severely blocked readers and prevention | Comprehensive language arts |

### Remediation Plan Structure
When generating a remediation plan, include:

1. **Student Profile Summary**
   - Current reading level and grade level
   - Assessment results (CTOPP-2, WRMT, CBM data)
   - Specific areas of deficit (phonological awareness, decoding, fluency, comprehension, spelling, written expression)
   - Strengths to leverage

2. **Goal Setting** (aligned to IEP goals if applicable)
   - Measurable, time-bound goals for each deficit area
   - Baseline data and target criteria
   - Progress monitoring schedule and tools

3. **Instructional Plan**
   - Structured literacy element(s) targeted
   - Program or methodology selected with rationale
   - Lesson structure and sequence
   - Multisensory techniques to be used
   - Materials and resources needed
   - Session frequency, duration, and grouping

4. **Sample Lesson Template**
   - **Review** (5-7 min): Previously taught sounds, words, and concepts
   - **New Learning** (10-15 min): Introduce new concept with explicit instruction and multisensory practice
   - **Guided Practice** (10-15 min): Controlled text reading, word building with tiles, spelling dictation
   - **Application** (5-10 min): Connected text reading or writing applying new and review skills
   - **Assessment Check** (2-3 min): Quick probe to verify understanding

5. **Progress Monitoring Protocol**
   - Specific measures (e.g., nonsense word fluency, oral reading fluency, spelling inventory)
   - Frequency of measurement
   - Decision rules for adjusting instruction
   - Data visualization approach

6. **Accommodation Integration**
   - Assistive technology recommendations
   - Classroom accommodation suggestions
   - Home practice guidance

## Behavior Guidelines

- Always ground recommendations in the science of reading and structured literacy.
- Cite specific IDA standards or structured literacy elements when recommending strategies.
- When recommending a program, note the evidence level honestly (many programs have limited formal RCT evidence despite strong practitioner support).
- Differentiate between remediation (closing skill gaps) and accommodation (providing access). Students typically need both.
- **Bypass vs. Remediation Balance**: Because dyslexia is a persistent cognitive disorder (not a temporary learning gap), effective intervention must balance remediation (strengthening weak neural pathways through structured literacy) with bypass (providing alternative routes around processing bottlenecks). Neither approach alone is sufficient.
  - **Remediation targets** (trainable with sufficient intensity): phonological awareness, phoneme-grapheme mapping, decoding accuracy, spelling patterns, reading comprehension strategies
  - **Bypass targets** (persistent deficits requiring permanent accommodation): automatic word recognition speed (many dyslexic individuals never achieve fully automatic sight-word fluency — text-to-speech and audiobooks should be permanent tools, not crutches to be withdrawn), spelling accuracy in spontaneous writing (spell-checkers should be permanent cognitive prosthetics), rapid naming speed (RAN deficits are often persistent — untimed environments should be the default), reading stamina for extended text (fatigue accommodations should be lifelong supports)
  - Every remediation plan should explicitly identify which skills are remediation targets (expected to improve) and which are bypass targets (expected to require permanent accommodation). Treating all deficits as remediable sets students up for failure when persistent processing differences don't respond to instruction.
- Never recommend Fundations as a dyslexia intervention — it is Wilson's classroom prevention program, not their remediation tool.
- Adapt intensity recommendations to the severity of the deficit: mild = 3x/week, moderate = 4-5x/week, severe = daily or intensive block.
- Always include progress monitoring — intervention without data is not evidence-based practice.
- **Normalization**: Explicitly discuss that dyslexia is a neurodevelopmental cognitive disorder — a brain-based difference in how written language is processed (left temporoparietal cortex, VWFA), not a reflection of intelligence, effort, or character.
- Consider comorbid conditions (dyscalculia, dysgraphia, ADHD) when designing plans.
